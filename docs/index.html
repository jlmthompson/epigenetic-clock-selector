How to use these recommendations

Recommendations are based on the training datasets, validation studies, and tissue/age coverage reported for each clock. No single clock is universally optimal — where possible, consider running 2–3 complementary clocks and reporting concordance. Discordant results can themselves be informative.

First-generation clocks (Horvath, Hannum) estimate biological age relative to chronological age and are best suited to cross-sectional studies. Second-generation clocks (PhenoAge, GrimAge, DunedinPACE) are trained on health outcomes and mortality, making them more appropriate when the primary interest is disease risk or response to intervention. Specialised clocks (PedBE, Knight, Lee) are designed for specific tissues or life stages and should be preferred in those contexts.

For a comprehensive review of epigenetic clock methodology and interpretation, see Horvath & Raj, Nat Rev Genet 2018.

Most clocks are implemented in R via the methylclock Bioconductor package, or via individual repositories linked in each clock card above.
